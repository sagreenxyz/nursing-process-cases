# Orem's Self-Care Deficit Theory Assessment

## Overview

**Dorothea Orem's Self-Care Deficit Nursing Theory** is one of the most widely used nursing theories. Orem views people as capable of self-care, but when self-care demands exceed self-care abilities, a **self-care deficit** exists - and that's when nursing is needed.

**Core Concepts:**
- **Self-Care:** Activities individuals perform to maintain life, health, and well-being
- **Self-Care Agency:** The person's ability to engage in self-care
- **Therapeutic Self-Care Demand:** All self-care actions required at a given time
- **Self-Care Deficit:** When self-care agency is inadequate to meet self-care demands
- **Nursing Agency:** The nurse's ability to help the patient meet self-care demands

**When Does Nursing Intervene?**
When self-care deficit exists - when the person **cannot** or **should not** meet their own self-care needs.

---

## Orem's Three Self-Care Requisites

### 1. Universal Self-Care Requisites
Basic needs ALL humans have (survival needs)

### 2. Developmental Self-Care Requisites
Needs related to life stage and developmental processes

### 3. Health-Deviation Self-Care Requisites
Needs arising from illness, injury, or medical treatment

---

## Orem's Three Nursing Systems

Orem identified three types of nursing systems based on the patient's self-care abilities:

1. **Wholly Compensatory:** Patient unable to perform any self-care (e.g., unconscious patient)
2. **Partly Compensatory:** Nurse and patient share self-care responsibilities
3. **Supportive-Educative:** Patient can perform care but needs teaching/support

For Mr. Martinez, we'll identify which system is needed for each self-care requisite.

## Orem's Nursing Process Application

### Step 1: Determine Why Nursing is Needed
**Self-care deficit exists:** Patient cannot meet self-care demands due to:
- Acute illness (limits abilities)
- Chronic illness (progressive limitations)
- Environmental barriers (transportation, finances, isolation)
- Knowledge deficits (some gaps)

### Step 2: Design Nursing System
**Partly Compensatory + Supportive-Educative systems** (combination)
- Acute phase: More compensatory
- Recovery phase: Transition to educative
- Post-discharge: Primarily educative with supports in place

### Step 3: Plan and Implement Interventions

**Acting or Doing For (Wholly Compensatory Methods):**
- Administer oxygen
- Administer medications
- Provide IV fluids
- Initially: Provide complete ADL care

**Guiding and Directing:**
- Teach COPD management
- Teach medication administration
- Teach symptom recognition
- Guide activity progression

**Providing Physical Support:**
- Assist with ADLs during acute phase
- Assist with mobility
- Provide positioning support

**Providing Psychological Support:**
- Emotional support for anxiety
- Support for grief and loss
- Encouragement

**Providing Developmental Environment:**
- Arrange support services
- Connect to resources
- Modify environment (remove barriers)

**Teaching:**
- COPD self-management
- Medication use
- Energy conservation
- Symptom monitoring
- When to seek help

**Question 3:** What's the difference between Orem's "partly compensatory" and "supportive-educative" systems, and why does it matter for discharge planning?

<details>
<summary>Click for answer</summary>

**Partly Compensatory:** Nurse does what patient cannot, patient does what they can. Both share responsibility.
- Example: Nurse provides IV meds, patient positions self for breathing

**Supportive-Educative:** Patient CAN perform care but needs teaching, guidance, or support.
- Example: Patient CAN take meds if barriers removed and taught proper technique

**Why this matters for discharge:**

If we discharge Mr. Martinez while he still needs **partly compensatory** care (can't perform basic self-care), he'll fail at home.

Before discharge, must transition to **supportive-educative** level - patient performs own care with:
- Education provided
- Barriers removed (med delivery, meals delivered, etc.)
- Support system in place
- Resources available

**Goal:** Patient leaves hospital able to perform self-care with supports in place, not still needing nurse to do it for him.
