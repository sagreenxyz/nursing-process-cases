## Framework #1: ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation)

### The Foundation of Prioritization

**Remember:** You can survive:
- ~3 minutes without oxygen
- ~3 days without water
- ~3 weeks without food

**Priority Order:**

### A = AIRWAY
**Is the airway open and patent?**

**Critical Questions:**
- Can air get in and out?
- Is there obstruction?
- Is the patient choking?
- Can they maintain their own airway?

**Life-Threatening Airway Problems:**
- Complete airway obstruction (choking)
- Severe swelling (anaphylaxis, angioedema)
- Blood/vomit in airway
- Loss of gag reflex with risk of aspiration
- Severe burns to airway

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
‚úì Airway is PATENT (he can speak, though limited)
- No obstruction
- Can cough (though weak)
- Gag reflex intact
‚Üí Move to B (Breathing)

---

### B = BREATHING
**Is the patient breathing adequately?**

**Critical Questions:**
- Is respiratory rate adequate? (Too fast? Too slow? Absent?)
- Is oxygenation adequate? (SpO2, color, mental status)
- Is breathing effective? (Depth, effort, accessory muscles)
- Is CO2 being eliminated? (ABG, mental status)

**Life-Threatening Breathing Problems:**
- Respiratory arrest
- Severe respiratory distress (impending failure)
- SpO2 < 90%
- Respiratory acidosis
- Pneumothorax
- Severe asthma/COPD exacerbation
- Pulmonary embolism

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
‚ö†Ô∏è Breathing is IMPAIRED
- SpO2 88% (critical)
- Respiratory rate 28 (tachypnea)
- Respiratory acidosis (pH 7.33, CO2 58)
- Severe dyspnea
- Accessory muscles
‚Üí **THIS IS THE PRIORITY** - Address before moving to C

---

### C = CIRCULATION
**Is perfusion adequate to maintain life?**

**Critical Questions:**
- Is there a pulse? (Adequate rate and quality)
- Is blood pressure adequate?
- Is there active bleeding?
- Are tissues being perfused? (Cap refill, skin color, urine output, LOC)
- Is the heart rhythm stable?

**Life-Threatening Circulation Problems:**
- Cardiac arrest
- Severe bleeding/hemorrhage
- Shock (cardiogenic, hypovolemic, septic, anaphylactic)
- Life-threatening dysrhythmias
- Severe hypotension
- Acute MI

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
‚úì Circulation is COMPENSATING
- HR 108 (tachycardia but adequate)
- BP 152/88 (elevated but perfusing)
- Pulses 2+ bilaterally
- Cap refill < 3 seconds
- Alert and oriented
‚Üí Circulation adequate FOR NOW (but monitor - tachycardia is compensatory)

---

### ABCs Applied: Mr. Martinez Priority Order

1. **BREATHING (Impaired Gas Exchange)** ‚Üê Address FIRST
   - SpO2 88%, respiratory acidosis, severe dyspnea
   - Could progress to respiratory arrest

2. **BREATHING (Airway Clearance)** ‚Üê Address SECOND
   - Excessive secretions impairing gas exchange
   - Could cause further respiratory compromise

3. **BREATHING (Breathing Pattern)** ‚Üê Address THIRD
   - Unsustainable respiratory effort
   - Patient will fatigue

4. **CIRCULATION** ‚Üê Monitor closely
   - Currently compensating
   - Could decompensate if breathing doesn't improve

## Framework #2: Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs

### The Human Needs Pyramid

```
                    SELF-
                 ACTUALIZATION
                (Achieving potential)
                       
                     ESTEEM
              (Confidence, respect)
                       
               LOVE & BELONGING
            (Relationships, connection)
                       
              SAFETY & SECURITY
         (Health, resources, stability)
                       
              PHYSIOLOGICAL
        (Air, water, food, shelter)
```

**Priority Rule:** Start at the BOTTOM (physiological) and work UP

---

### Level 1: PHYSIOLOGICAL NEEDS (Highest Priority)

**Basic survival needs - address FIRST**

**In Order of Urgency:**
1. **Oxygen** (minutes without = death)
2. **Circulation/Perfusion** (minutes to hours)
3. **Water/Fluid** (days without = death)
4. **Food/Nutrition** (weeks without = death)
5. **Elimination** (comfort, but less urgent)
6. **Temperature regulation** (hypothermia/hyperthermia)
7. **Rest/Sleep** (affects healing)

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
- Oxygen ‚úó (SpO2 88% - CRITICAL)
- Circulation ~ (compensating)
- Hydration ‚úó (dehydrated)
- Nutrition ‚úó (malnourished, but chronic)
- Elimination ‚úì (continent, adequate)
- Temperature ‚úó (fever - infection)
- Sleep ‚úó (disrupted, but not immediately dangerous)

**Priority:** Oxygen first, then hydration, then others

---

### Level 2: SAFETY & SECURITY

**Address AFTER physiological needs are met**

**Includes:**
- Freedom from physical harm (falls, injuries)
- Freedom from infection
- Pain management
- Medication safety
- Environmental safety

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
- Risk for Falls (high risk)
- Infection present (pneumonia - being treated)
- Pain (minimal - 2/10)

**Priority:** Prevent falls while treating respiratory issue

---

### Level 3: LOVE & BELONGING

**Address AFTER safety needs are met**

**Includes:**
- Social support
- Family involvement
- Communication with loved ones
- Feeling connected

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
- Lives alone, children out of state
- Social isolation

**Priority:** Important for discharge planning, but NOT while he's in respiratory failure

---

### Level 4: ESTEEM

**Address AFTER belonging needs are met**

**Includes:**
- Independence
- Dignity
- Self-care abilities
- Control over care

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
- Self-care deficits
- Loss of independence

**Priority:** Work toward this during recovery phase

---

### Level 5: SELF-ACTUALIZATION

**Address LAST**

**Includes:**
- Personal growth
- Achieving potential
- Meaningful activities
- Quality of life goals

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
- Ability to attend church
- Maintain hobbies
- Connect with grandchildren

**Priority:** Long-term goals after acute crisis resolved

## Framework #3: Urgent vs Important Matrix

### Eisenhower Decision Matrix

|  | **URGENT** | **NOT URGENT** |
|---|---|---|
| **IMPORTANT** | **QUADRANT 1**: DO FIRST<br/>Crises, life threats<br/>*Example: SpO2 88%* | **QUADRANT 2**: SCHEDULE<br/>Prevention, planning<br/>*Example: Discharge planning* |
| **NOT IMPORTANT** | **QUADRANT 3**: DELEGATE<br/>Interruptions, some requests<br/>*Example: Routine linen change* | **QUADRANT 4**: MINIMIZE<br/>Time wasters, busy work<br/>*Example: Reorganizing supply cart* |

### Applying to Mr. Martinez:

**QUADRANT 1 - Do Immediately:**
- Administer oxygen (SpO2 88%)
- Monitor respiratory status
- Assess ABG results
- Administer ordered medications
- IV fluid resuscitation

**QUADRANT 2 - Important but Can Wait:**
- Patient education (when stable)
- Discharge planning
- Case management consult
- Physical therapy consult
- Nutritional consult

**QUADRANT 3 - Delegate if Possible:**
- Vital signs (can delegate to CNA once stable)
- Assistance with ADLs (CNA)
- Meal tray delivery
- Transport to diagnostics (when stable)

**QUADRANT 4 - Minimize:**
- Non-essential documentation during crisis
- Social conversations
- Routine tasks that can wait

## Framework #4: Actual vs Risk vs Health Promotion

### Priority Order:

**1. ACTUAL Problems (Highest Priority)**
- Problems that EXIST RIGHT NOW
- Have assessment data proving they exist
- Require immediate intervention

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
- Impaired Gas Exchange (EXISTS - SpO2 88%)
- Deficient Fluid Volume (EXISTS - dehydrated)
- Anxiety (EXISTS - restless, fearful)

---

**2. RISK Problems (Medium Priority)**
- Problems that DON'T exist yet
- Patient is vulnerable
- Prevention is the goal

**Example - Mr. Martinez:**
- Risk for Falls (hasn't fallen, but high risk)
- Risk for Skin Breakdown (skin intact, but risk factors present)

---

**3. HEALTH PROMOTION (Lower Priority)**
- Patient is doing well
- Wants to do even better
- Focus on wellness

**Example:**
- Readiness for Enhanced Self-Management
(Not applicable during Mr. Martinez's acute crisis)

## Framework #5: Survival Potential

### Ask: "If I don't address this, will the patient survive?"

**WILL DIE if not addressed:**
- Airway obstruction
- Respiratory failure
- Cardiac arrest
- Severe bleeding
- Severe hypoglycemia
- Anaphylaxis

‚Üí **IMMEDIATE INTERVENTION**

**WILL DETERIORATE if not addressed:**
- Moderate respiratory distress
- Dehydration
- Infection
- Pain

‚Üí **PROMPT INTERVENTION** (within minutes to hours)

‚Üí **PLAN INTERVENTION** (within hours to days)

**WILL IMPROVE ON OWN OR WITH TIME:**

- Temporary limitations
- Expected post-procedure effects

‚Üí **MONITOR AND SUPPORT**

---

## Framework #6: Time-Sensitive Conditions

**"Time is Tissue"**

**For Mr. Martinez:**
- Respiratory failure ‚Üí Time-sensitive
- Sepsis risk ‚Üí Time-sensitive (antibiotics within 1 hour of sepsis recognition)

**Action:** IMMEDIATE intervention, don't delay

## Framework #8: Acute vs Chronic

### Priority Rule: ACUTE problems before CHRONIC

**ACUTE Problems:**
- Recent onset
- Can be resolved or improved quickly
- Often reversible
- Require immediate attention

**CHRONIC Problems:**
- Long-standing
- Managed, not cured
- Stable (usually)
- Important but less urgent

### Mr. Martinez Example:

**ACUTE (Priority):**
- Respiratory failure ‚Üê Address FIRST
- Pneumonia ‚Üê Address FIRST
- Dehydration ‚Üê Address FIRST
- Current anxiety episode ‚Üê Address FIRST

**CHRONIC (Important but Lower Priority):**
- COPD baseline ‚Üê Manage, but can't cure
- Malnutrition ‚Üê Long-term issue
- Social isolation ‚Üê Long-standing
- General anxiety ‚Üê Baseline trait

**Exception:** If chronic problem is causing acute crisis, address both
- Example: COPD (chronic) + exacerbation (acute) ‚Üí Treat exacerbation

---

## Framework #9: Systemic vs Local

### Priority: SYSTEMIC before LOCAL

**SYSTEMIC (Affects whole body):**
- Sepsis
- Respiratory failure (affects all organs via hypoxemia)
- Shock
- Electrolyte imbalances
- Acidosis/alkalosis

**LOCAL (Affects one area):**
- Wound
- Localized pain
- Peripheral IV infiltration
- Skin tear

### Mr. Martinez:

**SYSTEMIC:**
- Respiratory failure (all organs need oxygen) ‚Üê PRIORITY
- Infection (could become sepsis)
- Dehydration (affects multiple systems)

**LOCAL:**
- IV site (monitor, but not priority)

---

## Framework #10: Direct vs Indirect Problems

### Priority: DIRECT life threats before INDIRECT

**DIRECT Threats:**
- The problem itself is immediately dangerous
- Example: Airway obstruction, hemorrhage, MI

**INDIRECT Threats:**
- The problem leads to something dangerous
- Example: Immobility ‚Üí Risk for skin breakdown

### Mr. Martinez:

**DIRECT:**
- Impaired Gas Exchange ‚Üê The hypoxemia itself is dangerous
- Respiratory acidosis ‚Üê The pH itself is dangerous

**INDIRECT:**
- Immobility ‚Üí leads to ‚Üí Risk for skin breakdown
- Anxiety ‚Üí worsens ‚Üí Dyspnea

**Note:** Anxiety in this case is both direct (causes suffering) and indirect (worsens breathing). Address it early.

## Special Populations: Prioritization Adjustments

### Pediatrics
**Adjustments:**
- Small bodies decompensate FASTER
- Respiratory distress can progress to arrest quickly
- Dehydration more dangerous (smaller fluid reserves)
- **Priority:** Even more aggressive with ABC issues

---

### Geriatrics (Like Mr. Martinez)
**Adjustments:**
- May not show typical signs of distress
- Baseline may be different (lower BP, slower HR)
- Multiple chronic conditions complicate picture
- Falls are major risk
- **Priority:** Establish baseline, prevent decompensation, prevent falls

---

### Obstetrics
**Adjustments:**
- Two patients (mother and baby)
- Normal vital signs are different in pregnancy
- Certain conditions time-sensitive (eclampsia, abruption, hemorrhage)
- **Priority:** Consider impact on fetus

---

### Mental Health
**Adjustments:**
- Safety is paramount (suicide risk, violence risk)
- Physical health problems often present
- Don't assume symptoms are "just psychiatric"
- **Priority:** Safety first, then address acute physical needs

## Key Takeaways: Prioritization Principles

‚úì **ABC = Always Be Considering** Airway, Breathing, Circulation

‚úì **"What will kill my patient first?"** = Guide your priorities

‚úì **Actual problems before risk problems**

‚úì **Acute before chronic**

‚úì **Systemic before local**

‚úì **Address root causes** that worsen multiple problems

‚úì **Time-sensitive conditions** require immediate action

‚úì **Physiological needs** before psychosocial (Maslow)

‚úì **Priorities CHANGE** - reassess constantly

‚úì **One intervention can address multiple problems** (Example: O2 helps gas exchange, reduces anxiety, improves activity tolerance)

‚úì **Don't forget prevention** - Address risks before they become actual problems

‚úì **Use multiple frameworks** - They complement each other

‚úì **Trust your gut** - If something feels wrong, investigate immediately

‚úì **You can't do everything** - Delegate, ask for help, be realistic

‚úì **Documentation can wait** - Patient safety cannot
